	Confusion and uncertainty exits regarding the dates of some numbers of this serial.
	Michael Walters checked (2002.06.12) the copies there concerning the dates of
	Vol.XIII. His points are:
  • vol. 13 is stated to be for 1844-5, but the date of publication is given as 1845 on the title page.
  • In the introduction the editor(s) state that there had been a suspension of publication for financial reasons. Vol. 12 is dated 1840 and vol. 14 (a slim one) 1847.
  • Vol. 13 consists of two parts given on the tite page as parts 1 & 2, but in text as numbers 30 and 31. (vol. 12 has no indication of part number(s).)
    • Number 30 is dated April 1844
    • The caption "no. 30" appears on the top of the left-hand page throughout that number
    • Number 31 is dated December 1844.
    • "no. 31" is in the same position on the pages to the end of Jerdon's paper "Second supplement to the catalogue of the birds of Southern India", after that the pages bear no caption as to the part number.
    • There is a list of contents to no. 30 but not to no. 31.
  • All this suggests that the volume was not all printed at the same time, and that Jerdon's paper, though it ostensibly appeared in December 1844 was most probably not printed till 1845, presumably not till after August 9th. The Richmond Index indicates that no.31 while dated "April 1844" contains a reference to a paper of Aug. 9, 1845.

Notes:
  • A small volume exits that preceeds "Volume 1". This records a compilation of material from Aggasiz's library which served essentially as the nidus for the collection. [Not examined in any detail 2007.03 ].
  • Volumes 1 and 2 are logs of material accessioned in what appear to be two or three patterns.
    • Especially early on, significant portions of the accession are "block transfers" of material from the Aggasiz (L. & A.) libraries. I think there is essentially no "dating utility" to these entries, as the dates of publication are usually substantially prior to the accession dates.
    • Other material is entered (usually in red as opposed to black ink) and is subscriptions, purchased materials, exchanged materials, and materials donated by authors/institutions. The work pattern of entering these materials (where the greatest potential for "dating information" is to be found) apparently seems to be interrupted when large blocks of material are being transferred into the collection. Black ink is used for material donated by authors to the Museum or its members, and could in some instances provide very valuable information regarding dates, however. Some material has a quite regular and dependable pattern of appearance in the log. An excellent example of this is the American Naturalist, which often has accession dates that preceed what must be the imprint date. That is to say "no.2" is often accessioned in the very end of January for that year.
    • Some material is accessioned as bound volumes made up of a collection of individual articles, pertaining to a singe subject or group.
    • Additionally, scattered throughout are individual author's separates. I noticed a number of Cope separates, and these might be useful in demonstrating the dating of his material that in some cases preceeded the date of the published volume.

    • There is a fairly consistent discrepancy for plate numbers as given by the Richmond Index and other compilations. Daria Wingreen-Mason of the Cullman Library (SI) investigated this for me, by examining the copy in the holdings of the Cullman library; this was the copy that Richmond worked with. She writes (2008.08.04):
      • ~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~
It would appear the citation for Anthodiaeta zambesiana has been wrongly cited 
more than once because, as Dr. Donsker confirmed, the work was not paginated and the 
plates were not numbered.  Page numbers and plate numbers are referred to in 
extraneous parts of the book: the contents, list of plates, introduction, 
classification, and index; but not on the pages or plates themselves.  
Our copy appears to have been bound according to the information found in those 
finding aids and was later complicated due to misunderstanding and error on the 
part of the person who assembled our copy.  It is for this reason Richmond’s 
citation is debatable.  Let me explain part by part.   

·         The "Contents" (p. vi) states that Anthodiaeta zambesiana 
is found on page 343 and that hypodila can be found on page 345.  
In our copy the order was switched:  Zambesiana was stamped by someone "345", 
and hypodila was stamped "343".  Richmond likely cited the page as it was stamped.

·         The "List of Plates" (p. viii) states that plate 111 includes 
hypodila (figs. 1, 2) and zambesiana (fig. 3); 
and that plate 112 includes Arachnothera magna (fig. 1) & aurata (fig. 2).
In our copy the plates were numbered in pencil by hand.  
Unfortunately the numbering for plate 75 (which should have been Cinnyris coquereli) 
was misnumbered 76 and therefore threw off all subsequent plates.  
Were the sequences continued correctly (and cross checked with the list), 
then all the subsequent plates would match the "List of Plates", but instead 
the hand written numbering is one off.  Therefore, the plate number penciled 
in on the Anthodiaeta hypodila/zambesiana plate is 112, not 111.  
Again, it is likely that Richmond based his citation on an error.      

·         The "Classification" section in the prefatory parts (p. xlviii) 
cites Anthodiaeta zambesiana as being on p. 343 and hypodila on page 345.  
As mentioned earlier, in our copy the order was switched.   

·         The page order was probably switched to reflect how the birds 
appear on the plate itself.  Of the three figures on the plate, 
the Hypodila are not only the top two figures shown, but are also listed 
first in the caption below.  Interestingly, there are not figure numbers printed 
on the plate to identify the birds, one must use the bird descriptions to discern them.  
As you may recall, Hypodila were listed as fig. 1, 2. 

·         In the "Index" Anthodiaeta zambesiana is indexed under 
"Zambesi Collared Sun-Bird" and "zambesiana (Anthodiaeta)", both cited as pg. 343.

      	"1st portion of the 3rd part (Trochilidae) issued about Dec. 1860. 
	(Noted in Ibis , Jan, 1861, 108, as an early copy.)"
      Thus the publishing date is approximately 10 months later than the signature imprint date.

    • Further questioning of the date for vol.3 suggests that the entire volume may best be dated to 1863. Schulze et al. p.3050 date Rhopodytes from p.61 to 1863.
    • Date of Th.1
      • There is understandable controversy regarding the dates of Th.1; citation dates of 1850,1851, and 1852 can be found.
      • The imprint on the title has "1850-51".
      • Browning and Monroe studied this matter and concluded that it was "published" not as a complete volume, but in segments. They used signature numbers and dates in an attempt to establish the publishing history.
      • In 2006, Quaisser and Nicolai published "Typusexemplare der Vogelsammlung im Museum Heineanum Halberstadt", treating this matter in depth.
      • Quaisser and Nicolai note that the much discussed, and somewhat supported possibility that there were published preprints prior to 1851 could not be disproven. They go on to state (correctly in my view), that this segmented publication possibility remains to be proved, and the alternative scenarios involving non-published communications (e.g. remarks made in correspondence, or privately circulated printed matter) have not been disproved. Additionally they present evidence in support of these possibilities. They conclude (p.21) that adherence to the Code requires use of 1851, unless and until proof of partial publication prior to 1851 is established.
      • Some years later (2011) Dickinson, et. al in "Priority!" discusses this matter.
      • The conclusion (over Quaisser's and Dickinson's initials) is that it is "Arguably best dated 1851". (p.81).
      • Beneath the initialed section the "Notes" reach a different conclusion. The point turns on distinguishing proof sheets from final published material, and here (p.81) it is "postulated" that p.31 of the work was "part of a 4 page half gathering" and part of the publication. This postulation, combined with past "general accept[ence]" of the date, as well as considerations of "stability" prompt a conclusion that this speculative history of publication should be accepted. Thus while the matter is not resolved the choice is made to use speculation to support an earlier date.
      • My reading of the code does not support reliance on such speculation, and I follow both Quaisser &Nicolai, and the initial conclusion in "Priority!", that it is "best dated 1851". Within "Priority!" itself, the work is cited to 1851 (see for example p.38).
