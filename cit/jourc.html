  • Sherborn Ann.Mag.Nat.Hist.(10) 1928 "XXII. Dates of Publication of Catalogues of Natural History (post 1850) issued by the British Museum." 13:308-312 publishes dates of publication that are at variance with those published by Mathews.
  • Mathews in his 1925 Bibliography of the Birds of Australia pp.25-6 gives dates and refers in text to the "... Ascertained date when secured." Presumably this date is that which is listed in the table as "Actual Date". (Though one must note that in the text just above he states "The exact dates of publication are not yet know." So in just three lines we have "exact dates", "Ascertained dates" (very peculiar that it is capitalized), and "Acutal Date." Much of Mathews' writing and work appears to me to have suffered mightily from having been edited by himself.
  • CWR list vol.6 as "1881" and dates 1882.
  • Dates of issue in BBOC 9:1 gives 1881 for vol.6.
  • Letter from Mrs F E Warr 94.12.13 The Nat. Hist Mus. Tring:
    "Rex Banks has passed me your query about the date of publication of volume 6 of the Catalogue of Birds in the British Museum." "I have looked at all our copies here but the only clue I can find is that one copy was accessioned on 16th January 1882. With Sharpe's Introduction dated 26th December it's therefore somewhere between those two dates."
  • Regarding vol.XX, Zimmer (p.97) notes (quotes are his)
    		It was completed in December last, and is dated 1891, though it was not
		actually accessible to the public until the middle of last March" (Ibis, July 1892).
    (Thanks to James Jobling via Edward Dickinson for bringing this to my attention). Interpretation here is problematic, and either 1891 or 1892 may be correct. It is not impossible that it may have had limited, internal distribution. My initial interpretation was that this date should be changed to 1892, thinking that a speculative scenario was necessary to consider the work as "published" in 1891. However, on reflection I consider that the information in the Ibis speaks not to "publication" but to "accessib[ility] to the public." As is often the case I get the impression that either the Code is poorly written, or my intellect is not sufficient to the task of its interpretation. I suppose a combination of the two is the most likely.

    	In 1926, C. Davies Sherborn, Ann Mag Nat Hist ser. 9, vol.
	17:271-272 published a brief note titled: Dates of Publication of
	Early Catalogues of Natural History issued by the British Museum. In that
	work Sherborn attributes the Catal. Spec. and Drawings .... by Hodgson to
	G. R. Gray, 1846 with the bracketed date [9 January 1847].  In the text he
	states that 'Information supplied from the Records by permission of the
	Trustees of the British Museum.'  Further, he stated that many difficulties
	have arisen in question of priority and that it is impossible to recover the
	exact date they were offered for public sale. He continues that " ... we
	must be contented to recover the exact day on which they were 'laid on the
	table' of the Trustees, a preliminary to the issue to the public.' He
	notes that these are the dates he used in the Index Animalium.

    • There has been controversy, confusion, and uncertainty regarding how the last "portion" of this work should be dated. 1850 or 1851. In their address to this problem Browning & Monroe Arch.Nat.Hist. 1991 18(3):382 state: 
      	The date 10 November 1850 is printed on page 537 of the last signature.
	The year cited for several taxa, for example Arremonops conirostris 
	Bonaparte and other taxa (See A.O.U., 1983: 670, 682, 698, etc.), should
	be 1850 rather than 1851.
      This suggests to me that Browning & Monroe are basing their date of the last portion (at least p.465-543, and possibly p.401-543) on a signature date, which I would regard as unsatisfactory as a method of establishing a date of publication.
    • What to do? In my view, a reasonable argument would be to place the emphasis on the date specified (i.e. the date imprinted on the title page), 1850 for vol. I. First, clearly some material was in fact published in 1850. For all material from Volume I, then 1850 should be accepted unless one can establish proof of delay into 1851 for publication of part of the material. Notification of receipt by the Paris Academy of the last part of vol. I on Feb. 3 1851 is evidence for, but certainly not proof of delay in publication. Of note in this regard, is that quite clearly the notification of receipt by the Paris Academy appears to have been at least several weeks later than the actual time of publication. This appears to be demonstrated by the Paris Academy noticing the receipt of some materail 6 days (virtually a week) after it's receipt was noted in Philadelphia. So the Paris Academy notification in this instance appears to have occured an absolute minimum of two weeks after the actual date of publication, and more likely it was three, 4, or more weeks delay.
    • The dates of portions of the last part of the work are of some controversy, interest and importance. Zimmer discusses this, and indicates support for the understanding that the final portion was distributed (privately) in parts -- though the completed version of the final portion was not widely available until 1857. 	Murray Bruce has examined the question of these dates further and in some detail.
	He determines that the last part of the work (from pp. 141-162) 
	was all that was issued in 1853.  Murray kindly forwarded the results 
	of his work on this problem, and I include his discussion here:
	
    
	
      

	
    • "I discussed this publication [Contrib.Orn.] earlier on an owl name.  
	I then pointed out that the final volume of Jardine's series, Contr. Orn.,  
	was intended for 1852 but was delayed until 1853 (recall I also pointed out 
	earlier that I had copies of title pages for both 1852 and 1853).   
	The series, begun in 1848, was intended to cover each year with new material 
	and reviewing some activities of the year in question.  
	The series was under the influence of Jardine's son-in-law, Hugh Strickland, 
	who had been an active observer and critic of ornithological developments (see my foreward).  
	His accidental death in 1853 ended the series - Jardine (and his daughter) 
	never completely recovered from this tragedy.
      

	However, after my earlier discussion on the change of date with the owl name (cf. Tyto glaucops), 
	I also intended to look further into the matter[, and ...] did so, and I think I have finally sorted 
	out the problem - and I was wrong with the owl name - yes, it is 1852!  
	What tends to be overlooked is that the 'Contributions' were published in parts, 
	but apparently no details have been found on this aspect of the publication.  
	For example, in the 1852 vol. on p.134 is a letter dated Oct. 1852 relating to a point discussed on p.78 
	of the same volume.  It doesn't seem to be an issue from a nomenclatural point of view 
	(but I haven't had time to check this).  In the case of the final volume the confusion 
	seems to stem from the fact that all but the final part came out in 1852.
       

	What page marks the beginning of the final part from 1853?  
	I have found the answer. Hartlaub's series of reviews of the literature covering each year from 1846 
	to 1871 solves the problem.  I was reminded of this work when I used one of his reviews for the foreward.  
	In the coverage of 1853 literature he mentioned that he received the final part and that the tragic death 
	of Strickland marked the end of the series.  By checking his listings under regions and families for the 
	volume in his 1852 and 1853 reviews, I found that the final part contained only two articles on pp.141-162.  
	The first is a report on a collection of birds from SW Africa by Strickland and Sclater 
	(the new birds but one named by Strickland alone); the second a brief note translating the description of a 
	new hornbill named by Sundevall. 
        

	As I have a copy of the volume, I went through the relevant text to see what was done in Peters and found this:
	
      	2:339 - 1852		5:200 - 1852		6:118 - 1852 (1853)
	9:22 - '1852-1853'	9:26 - 1852 (1853)	9:47 - 1853, with vol. as '1850-52'
	10:98 - 1852		11:36 - 1852		11:163 - 1852
	15:93 - 1852
	
      

	Apart from wondering why Hartlaub was overlooked (although further checking may reveal others had followed him) 
	it was obviously convenient to cite the whole volume as 1852, as with the previous ones for their respective years.  
	From the above and your query on names from Peters 7:24,39, it would appear that between vols 5 & 6 Peters 
	was led to believe the 1852 volume was 1853 and changed the citations accordingly.  The three citations to vol.9 
	are to lark names and while listed inconsistently, they all indicate 1853.  I assume this is because the family 
	account was originally done by Peters and the final version suggests editorial 'adjustments' to Peters's MS.
        

	It would seem that either Peters himself or someone else he knew misinterpreted the publication of the whole 
	volume as 1853.  However it happened, Peters used 1853 consistently in the remainder of the series he prepared 
	himself. Looking at a subsequent checklist for southern Africa, in this case the first one by Clancey in the 1960s, 
	he has the lark names as 1852, presumably correcting 'errors' in Peters, although the woodpecker from vol.6 got 
	through as 1853.
      

	Anyhow, the end result is that the names you queried should be 1852, with 1853 only applying to the names 
	indicated above.  While some have already been covered in HBW, note those where this can be corrected.
