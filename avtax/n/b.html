Newtonia brunneicauda Citation
  • Peters Checklist XI:206 (= Traylor 1986) gives the citation as:
    Erythrosterna? brunneicauda  A. Newton 1863 Proc. Zool. Soc. London p. 180
    and indeed I believe the description and the name do occur there.
  • However, this is Part II (Part I includes pp.1-96) and Part II was published in October of 1863. The name and the diagnosis were also included in the Ibis that year; they are found in the July issue (no.XIX). The July publication has precedence over the October publication.
  • The confusion here is understandable, because in the Ibis article E. Newton refers to the name (attributed to his brother A. Newton) and cites "Proc. Zool. Soc., May 12, 1863*" and in a footnote the Editor "subjoins" A. Newton's the name and description as quoted text.
  • It is easy to see how this creates the impression that the PZS material had already been published.
  • I think it is clear that the "May 12, 1863" refers to the date of meeting where the material was presented, not when it was published.
  • The only other possible explanation is that the July Ibis was delayed, and not published until after Part II of the 1863 PZS had been published (in October). To date there is no evidence of such a delay in the Ibis.
  • Special thanks to Michael Rieser for picking this up.

Zosterops borbonicus Citation
  • Conventionally cited to Gmelin, 1789 (e.g. Peters Checklist 12:333; H&M 3rd:630). Though these authorities make this citation without discussion or supporting arguments. It is my interpretation that despite the renderings of the above authorities, the available published material makes it clear that Forster's name has priority.

buttikoferi Nomenclature (epithet spelling)
  • The Usual original spelling is büttikoferi.
  • Most authors currently spell this buettikoferi, presumably either simply following what others have done, or in the belief that they are following the Code.
  • The relevant text of the Code is:
    	32.5.2.1. In the case of a diacritic or other mark, the mark concerned is deleted, 
	except that in a name published before 1985 and based upon a German word, the umlaut sign 
	is deleted from a vowel and the letter "e" is to be inserted after that vowel 
	(if there is any doubt that the name is based upon a German word, it is to be so treated).
    .
  • So the question becomes: Is büttikoferi based on a German word?
  • This, of course, is an honorific eponym for Dr Johann Büttikofer. Wikipedia (2009.01.25) tells us this:
    	Dr. Johann Büttikofer (August 9, 1850 - June 24, 1927), was a Swiss zoologist.

	Büttikofer was born in Ranflüh im Emmenthal and studied biology at Berne. 
	In 1879 he joined the staff of the Royal Zoological Museum in Leiden, becoming 
	curator between 1884 and 1897. He made two trips to Liberia, the first was from 1879 to 1882, 
	and the second from 1886 to 1887. In 1893-94 he accompanied the Nieuwenhuis expedition to central Borneo.

	Between 1897 and 1924 he was Director of the Zoological Garden at Rotterdam. After his retirement he settled in Berne.
  • So the name is a Swiss name, not a German name. The Swiss that I know would not be happy about being considered to be "German".
  • Is there evidence that this Swiss name is "based on a German word"? I do not find such evidence. The only German words I find in my Langenscheidt German Dictionary (1973) that start with "Bü..." are Büttel [= bailiff or beadle], and Büttenpapier [= handmade paper]; Büttenrand [= deckle edge]; I don't see those as applying here.
  • Büttikofer, being Swiss, is absolutely not itself a "German" name, and I find no support for the idea that it is "based upon a German word".
  • By my interpretation of the Code, the specific epithets must be spelled buttikoferi, being based upon a Swiss name.
  • If compelling evidence is brought forward that Büttikofer is based on a German word (a definitional problem all of its own), then the "-ue-" spelling will be appropriate.
  • If the argument is made that "Swiss and German is the same thing", I think the Swiss will be very unhappy.

Microscelis amaurotis batanensis Citation
  • This name was published simultaneously by McGregor and Mearns in the Oct. 1907 issue of the Phillipine Journal of Science.
  • Mearns' type is now in the U.S.Natl.Mus.; Deignan ("Type specimens of birds in the United States National Museum". 1961 p.366) indicates that McGregor's use of the name was based on 18 specimens in the collection of the Philippine Bureau of Science.
  • Dickinson et al.. 2002. Zool.Verh. Leiden 340:115-160 discuss these two names and dismiss the McGregor name stating he "gave no description of his own, merely citing a character given by Mearns and declaring that he could not see this." They go on to indicate that they feel that "Art. 1.3.1 of the Code would seem to apply (ICZN, 1999)."
  • Article 1.3.1 of the Code Excludes names proposed "for hypothetical concepts" and an hypothetical concept is defined in the Glossary as 
    	"A taxonomic concept that when published contained no animal 
	then known to exist in nature, past or present, but only in the 
	mind of the author whether a prediction or not."
    which I think clearly does not apply here.
  • Additionally it must be noted that the representation by Dickinson et al. clearly does not represent the situation. McGregor did not "merely cit[e] a character given by Mearns and declar[e] that he could not see this." He cited a character misdescribed by Mearns, and thus was correct in "not see[ing] it.", but agreed that one of the characters proposed by Mearns was present and was, he believed sufficient to serve as a basis for distinguishing the taxon.
  • The Richmond Index indicates that McGregor's work has "Little descr. here." though how much, and whose this is, is not clear.
  • Not currently having seen the original descriptions, it appears likely to me that Mearns should stand as the author based on article 50.1.1 which states:
    50.1.1. However, if it is clear from the contents that some person other than an author of the 
work is alone responsible both for the name or act and for satisfying the criteria of 
availability other than actual publication, then that other person is the author of the 
name or act. If the identity of that other person is not explicit in the work itself, 
then the author is deemed to be the person who publishes the work.
  • It is further interesting that Deignan (1961 p.366) goes on to correct Mearns' description of the bird!!

Basilinna Systematics
  • The relationship between Basilinna and Hylocharis is of interest. As partial history is as follows. Previously
    1. Basilinna was employed for leucotis and xantusi in the 2nd AOU CL (1895).
    2. Basilinna was used as a subgenus of Hylocharis in the 4th AOU CL (1931).
    3. this continued in the 5th AOU CL (1957).
    4. In the 6th AOU CL (1983), Basilinna was listed as a synonym of Hylocharis (subgenera not being used).
    5. In 1995 Howell & Webb "A guide to the birds of Mexico and N. Central America" Oxford Press (not seen) Evidently put leucotis and xantusi back in Basillina at genus level.
    6. HBW 5 appears to follow Howell & Webb's treatment.
  • Thus, there does not appear to be a good published basis for this treatment. However, unpublished molecular work, as well as studies of the birds themselves are said to support holding Basillina as distinct from Hylocharis (and evidently not even it's sister taxon, despite it's previously being held to be a subgenus of it).
  • The use of this genus thus is labelled "tentative" by H&M 3rd:268.

Pseudocolaptes boissonneautii Nomenclature
  • Originally spelled Anabates boissonneautii (note the "t").
  • Normand David has examined the original description and writes:
    
The original is indeed boissonneautii, but the authors write:  "nous la dédions à M.
Boissonneau." (we dedicate the name to Mr. Boissonneau).
  • The Code (1999) indicates (Article 32.5)
    32.5. Spellings that must be corrected (incorrect original spellings).

	32.5.1. If there is in the original publication itself, without recourse to any 
	external source of information, clear evidence of an inadvertent error, such as a 
	lapsus calami or a copyist's or printer's error, it must be corrected. 
	Incorrect transliteration or latinization, or use of an inappropriate connecting vowel, 
	are not to be considered inadvertent errors.
    My initial interpretation was that under the current Code the species group name must be corrected to boissonneauii; arguing that boissonneautii is an "incorrect latinization", and thus requiring it remain the same, seemed unsupportable.
  • However, Normand David reflects further on this problem, and writes (2006.02.09):
    Note that art. 31.1.1 gives much latitude to authors. For  example, the name Margaret can be latinized to
Margarita or Magaretha,  yielding the genitives margaritae or margarethae. Now, can we have
Boissonneau = boissonneautius = boissonneautii ?  The more I think  about it, the more I am
inclined to say that "boissonneautii" may not  be correctible.
  • The Code, in my interpretation, requires that the decision turns on distinguishing between two kinds of error: an "error of latinisation" versus a typographic error or lapsus calami. It must be noted that the Code provides no guidlines, directions, or instructions on how these two types of errors are to be distinguished. I am amused to speculate as to why no guidelines or further instructions are offered for making this determination. However, as no charitable explanations come to mind I will refrain from including the possibilities that occur to me.

Bubo blakistoni Citation
  • Usually (e.g. Peters Checklist 4:121; HBW 5:193,687) cited as:
    • Bubo blakistoni Seebohm 1884 PZS(1883) p.466
  • The Richmond Index listing gives: Seebohm Ibis ser.5 Vol.II no.V Jan 1884 p.42
  • A note added to the Richmond Index card says: 'First "descr." in PZS "Nov. 1883", 466.' [The quotation marks are, I believe, indicative of Richmond's skepticism regarding the truth of the particulars as stated.]
  • For something to be published on p.466 (Pt4) of the PZS in 1884, the earliest it could have been published would be April, which would give the Jan. 1884 Ibis publication priority.
  • Therefore, contra to Peters and HBW, I follow the Richmond Index and list the Seebohm Ibis, January 1884 citation.
