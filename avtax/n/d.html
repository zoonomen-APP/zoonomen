2020.12.01
Diuca Systematics / Nomenclature
  • The current version (10.2) of the IOC World Bird Checklist holds Diuca to be monotypic contatining Diuca diuca.
  • Some (Peters (=Paynter) and Hellmayr) held the type of Diuca to be while Sharpe (CBBM 12, held the type to be Fringilla diuca Molina.
  • Terry Chesser provides the resolution, writing: "This appears to be based on Burns et al. (2016), who stated the following: 'The necessity of allocating diuca and speculifera to different genera reopens the question of the type species of Diuca because the species that is not the type may require a new genus. The issue is resolved by Article 67.2 of the International Code of Zoological Nomenclature (ICZN 1999), hereafter the Code. In the first explicit assignment of species to either of the relevant genera Diuca Reichenbach and Hedyglossa Cabanis, Cabanis (1851: 135) listed only one, Fringilla diuca Molina, 1782, which applies to both generic names, irrespective of Cabanis’s treating Diuca as a synonym of Hedyglossa. Under Article 67.2.2 of the Code, Fringilla diuca thus becomes the only species eligible for fixation as the type for either of these generic names, effectively by monotypy (Article 69.3). Consequently, Gray’s (1855) subsequent designation of speculifera as type species of Diuca is irrelevant, and if speculifera is to be assigned to a monospecific genus, a new generic name is needed for it. Accordingly we provide Chionodacryon here.'"
  • Chesser also notes that Bonaparte 1850, Consp.Gen.Avium may constitute the typification, in which case possibly three taxa are available for the choice of the type.

Pomatorhinus erythrogenys dedekeni Nomenclature
  • The original spelling was Pomatorhinus Dedekensi and this is conventionally corrected to dedekeni -- having been named in honor of Father Dedeken.
  • This change is correct only if Father Dedeken is explicitly and correctly referenced in the article containing the original description. I assume this is so, but do not know it to be true for certain.
  • (2009.11.27) Theo de Kok helpfully points out that the original description mentions Father Dedekens (sic). even though the gentleman's name was "Dedeken".
  • The original spelling must stand.
  • Interestingly, Edward Dickinson notes from his communications with Bert Deignan, that Bert was aware of this problem and apparently wrestled with the decision as to how to proceed.

Amazilla decora Systematics
  • The question here is should this taxon be held to be a full species or a subspecies.
  • Treatment as a subspecies:
    • Peters 5:66 (1945)
    • Wolters lief.2 p.131 (1976)
    • SACC citing Peters, and "Weller (1999)" [?=HBW 5:601] in support.
    • H&M 3rd:266 with a note:
      Type species of the genus Polyerata still submerged here,
	pending further publication.
      The full implications of that note are not clear to me.

  • Treatment as a species:
    • AOU 7th CL p.298 (1989).
    • Cory 1918 (not seen).
  • Weller's note in HBW 5:601 states: 
    Race decora sometimes considered a separate species on grounds
mainly of morphological differences, combined with distribution.
    No data or argument is offered why that is insufficient for holding it to be a full species.
  • Consideration of this matter is currently best summarized in an email from F. Gary Stiles (2006.06.30):
    ... I considered it a separate species in the Birds of Costa Rica because of
differences in plumage, to which might be added differences in body proportions, song 
behavior and to some extent, ecology. My feeling then and now is that superspecies status 
for decora+amabilis, with each being considered an allospecies, is most appropriate. 
The avifauna of the Pacific slope of S Costa Rica and extreme SW Panamá contains 
a number of forms that differ from their closest relatives on the Atlantic slope, 
decora vs. amabilis being one such case. These two show differences comparable 
to those of other such forms currently considered (allo)specifically distinct
in genera as diverse as Cotinga, Carpodectes, Manacus, Thamnophilus or Thryothorus,
hence I can see no problem in doing likewise here.

Caprimulgus rufigena damaresis Citation
  • The date of this portion of Contrib.Orn. has been a difficult problem, and both uncertainty and different opinions as to how to proceed remain.
  • Following the treatment by Murray Bruce (based on Hartlaub's notice of this material for the year 1853) and on Richmond's writing a "3" above the "1852" in his Index, I chose to date this material to 1853.

Phaenicophaeus diardi Citation date
    Voisin & Voisin. 1999. "Liste des types d'oiseaux des collections du Muséum national d'Histoire naturelle de Paris. 6. Coucous (Cuculidae)" Zoosystema 21(2):379-402. Date this taxon to 1831.
  • This publication was issued in parts, with Livraison 2 published May 8, 1830. The work as a whole, which I imagine the Voisons examined, has an imprint date of 1831, so it is easy for the casual worker to be mislead.
  • Sherborn's Index Animalium (p.1900; 1925), Peters Checklist 4:51 (1940) and HBW 4:573 (1997) have the correct date of 1830. Cat.B.Br.Mus. 19:390 has the date incorrect as 1831.
  • This is not the only dating mistake made by Voisin & Voisin; they replicate this error with other Lesson taxa:
    1. Bubatus isidori
    2. Bubatus duvaucelii
    3. Centropus pumilus
    all of which should be 1830 and not 1831.

Chersophilus duponti Citation
  • Cited by Peters Checklist 9:54 (Mayr & Greenway) as published in 1820, and this is followed by H&M 3rd:547.

To evaluate this possibility I looked at the synonomy in CBBM 13:526 (Sharpe). It appear that according to Sharpe the next available name would be a Temminck 1835 name Certhilauda duponti -- a name not found in Sherborn or the Richmond Index.

Dictionnaire Universal d' Histoire Naturelle Citation
  • Works from volume 1 of this work are cited by Peters Checklist 7:124,127 as having been published in 1840. (Note here that the orthographic rendering in this volume of Peters Checklist would appear to indicate the imprint date with no parentheses, and the publication date with parentheses).
  • The HBW 8:328,338 (Remsen) confuses both the Peters Checklist representation (which is confusing) as well as the dates from the work in which these taxa are described, listing the date as "1842". Of additional interest the date "1842" evidently NEVER occurs as an imprint date (!), though it is the actual date of completion of volume 2.
  • These confusions are reproduced without comment or discussion in H&M 3rd:414,416.
  • The dates of publication of this work were dealt with in detail by Sherborn and Palmer in 1899. Sherborn CD, Palmer TS. 1899. "Dates of d'Orbigny's 'Dictionnaire Universel d'Histored Naturelle,' 1839-1849." Ann.Mag.Nat.Hist.(7) 3:350-352. Sherborn and Palmer's treatment is reflected in subsequent compilations (e.g. Sherborn's Index Animalium, Schulze et al., Neave), and those treatments do not seem to have been consulted by subsequent workers.
  • Of interest, Sclater in Cat.BirdsBrit.Mus. 15:105 cites the date 1847 for the genus Anabazenops. 1847 is the imprint date on the title page of the copy in the Zoological Society Library (the B.M. (N.H.) copies have imprint dates of 1861 for this volume). Certainly the most valuable copy, as noted by Sherborn and Palmer, is the copy then in the U.S. National Museum, which was Baird's personal copy and has an imprint date of 1841 for the first volume. 1841 is the date of the completed volume, with the individual livraisons starting to be issued in Dec. 1839.
  • Also of note, Sibley & Monroe 1990 is alone in correctly interpreting the dates from Peters, giving the date as "1840" for Anabacerthia striaticollis (p.407) even though that Peters interpretation of the date is wrong. Sibley & Monroe, it appears is not consulted by current workers, even though much of the bibliographic work in it is of very high quality.

Dromaius Nomenclature
  • Originally spelled "Dromiceius" on p.54 of Analyse, and spelled "Dromaius" on p.70 in the listing of "Nouveaux noms, tirés du grec, ..." [trans. New names, coming from the Greek], where the listing reads:
  • "Dromaius [δρομαιος, velox]."
    • Notes:
    • δρομαιος [Greek, trans. running at full speed, swift, fleet];
    • velox [Latin, trans. swift, rapid, quick, fleet].
  • The consistent (and reasonable) interpretation has been that the original spelling was a misprint, or printer's error.
  • In much of the 19th and early 20th century the name was spelled Dromaeus (e.g. Cat.B.Br.Mus.) where the listing reads:
    		1. DROMÆUS
	Dromiceius (errore), Vieill. Analyse, p.54 (1816) .. D. novæ hollandiæ Type.
	Dromaius, Vieill. op. cit. p.20 (1816); id. N. D. [APP note: ?misprint for p.70?]
		x. p.211 (1817).
	Dromæus, Ranz. El. di Zool. iii. 1 p.88 (1821) [APP note: ? misprint for p.98 or p.99?]
	Tachea, Flem. Phil. of Zool. ii. p.257 (1822)  .... D. novæ hollandiæ.
	Dromiceus, Sw. Class B. ii. p.346 (1837)
	Dromeicus, A. Newton, Dict. of B. p.213(note)
		(pt. 1, 1893)
  • Alfred Newton, 1896, in his Dictionary of Birds (p.213) includes this note:
    1 The obvious misprint of Dromeicus in this author's work (Analyse &c.,
p.54) has been foolishly followed by many naturalists, forgetful that he corrected 
it a  few pages further on (p.70) in Dromaius - the properly latinized from 
of which is Dromæus.
    [APP: one has to chuckle at the fact that Newton himself misspells Vieillot's original misspelling.]
  • As noted by the Cat.B.Br.Mus. listing the Dromæus / Dromaeus rendering is an emendation attributable to Camillio Ranzani. 1821. The Richmond Index provides two listings for this:
    • Dromaeus Elementi di Zoologia, III, Parte 1, p.99
    • Dromoeus Elementi di Zoologia, III, Parte 1, p.98
    So Ranzani, along with Vieillot, and Newton fights for consistency with limited success.
  • Ernst Mayr's note in Peters Checklist 1979 1(2):9 reads:
     3The incorrect formation Dromiceius, possibly arising from a
printer's misreading of the manuscript, and changed to Dromaius by 
the author a few pages later, was not employed except as a synonym
of Dromaius until early in the twentieth century. G. R. Gray, as 
first reviser, 1840, List Gen. Birds, p.63, clearly established Dromaius
as the correct spelling. Cf. Serventy, Condon, and Mayr, 1965, Bull. 
Zool. Nomencl., 22, pp.63-65, and  Melville, 1977, Bull. Zool. Nomencl.,
34, pp.12-13. --E. M.
    I am dissapointed that Mayr does not discuss the use of Dromaeus as prevalent througout the latter 19th and early 20th centuries. It does seem reasonable to accept his presentation that Gray, as first reviser, fixes Dromaius as the name.

Megalaima haematocephala delica Nomenclature
  • Two points of consideration:
    1. Date:
      • Listed by Peters Checklist 6:40 as published in 1907, and this is followed by H&M 3rd:306 and HBW 7:209.
      • For this volume of Abh.K.Bay.Akad.Wiss. that would seem to be several years too early.
      • The Richmond Index lists the date as 1910 which appears much more reasonable for this volume. I previously followed the Richmond Index here.
      • I have come to understand from Edward Dickinson, that there is unpublished data that indicates that this was published in a separate in 1907, though I have no independant confirmation of this fact.
      • Edward Dickinson writes (2004.10.02):
        	[Murray Bruce appears] to have an original separate dated 1907. 
	In the Rothschild library there are two copies, the title
	page is dated 1907 and the wrapper present in one case is also dated 1907. 
	The whole volume has been examined, in South Kensington, [where] the title page
	for the volume is 1910; the title page for part I is dated 1906, but at the end
	of this, the second article in Part I, it reads "Angegeben am 6 November 1907";
	the BMNH date stamp shows receipt on 26 November 1907.
        The case for 1907 being correct appears to be made.
    2. Nomenclature:
      • Originally described in Megalaema
      • Peters Checklist 6:40 puts the author's name in parentheses, interpreting Megalaema to be different from Megalaima. This is followed by HBW 7:209 and H&M 3rd:306, and in neither case does this appear to warrant discussion.
      • ICZN 1999 51.3.1 states:
          
51.3.1 Parentheses are not used when the species-group name was originally
combined with an incorrect spelling or an emendation of the generic name (this
applies even though an unjustified emendation is an available name with its own
authorship and date [Art.  33.2.3]).
      • Thus it appears that at least HBW and H&M consider Megalaema to be something other than an emendation or incorrect spelling.
      • To date, I am unable to find any indication that Megalaema was proposed as a distinct generic entity. Thus I interpret it to be an emendation and do not use parentheses around the author's name. Sherborn lists Megalaema (without ligature) as an Agassiz "emend. pro Megalaima, Gray.", and Schulze "Nomen. animalium gen. et subgen." p.1995 also has this interpretation.
      • If Megalaema was proposed distinctly as a genus, and is not a misspelling or emendation I would be very eager to know of the facts supporting that case as it appears to be unknown to Sherborn, Richmond, or Schulze, et al.

Ferret and Galinier's 'Voyage en Abyssinie'; Lefebvre's Voyage en Abyssinie'; 'Exploration scientifique de l'Algérie'; Castelnau's 'Amérique du Sud'; Dumont d'Urville's 'Voyage de l'Astrolabe'; Laplace's 'Voyage sur la Favorite'; Jacquemont's Voyage dans l'Inde'; Tréhouart's 'Commission scientifique d'Islande'; Cailliaud, 'Voyage à éroé'; 'Expédition scientifique de Mor´e'; Fabre, 'Commission scientifique du Nord'; Du etit-Thouars, 'Voyage de la Vénus' and on the dates of the 'Faune Française'. -- Ann. Perdix dauurica Nomenclature
  • This specific epithet is spelled in a variety of ways. From 1976 to 1996 the Zoological Record includes these spellings:
    • Perdix daurica
    • Perdix dauricae
    • Perdix dauuriae
    • Perdix dauurica
    • Perdix dauuricae
    • Perdix dauuricus
  • Peters Checklist 2:89 gives the original combination as: Tetrao perdix var. daurica [sic] Pallas.
  • Vaurie (1965) used dauurica.
  • McGowan in HBW 2:505 gives the original combination as:
    • P. d. dauurica (Pallas 1811)
    and used dauuricae.
  • Sibley & Monroe 1990 p.15 used "dauuricae", but in the supplement S&M 1993 p.16 wrote: "The correct spelling of the specific name is Perdix dauurica (Stepanyan 1990a)".
  • Tetrao perdix is described on p.77 of Pallas' 1811 work, and on p.78 (where "dauuricae" appears) the representation is
    • "Varietatis rupestris dauuricae".
    The first and third words are in italics. A ten line description follows.
  • I quote Normand David's discussion of the grammar that applies here:
    "Pallas's wording is "Varietatis rupestris dauuricae". The word varietatis is a feminine noun in the genitive case, and dauuricae is the adjective dauuricus (-a, -um) in the feminine form of the genitive case. The word rupestris is a Modern Latin adjective (derived from the classical noun rupes: rock, mountain, cavern) in the genitive case (the nominative is identical). The literal translation is:
    • in French: "de la variété rupestre daourienne"
    • in English: "of the Daurian rupestrian variety".
    IF dauuricae Pallas 1811 is available, then it falls under ICZN (Code 1999) Art. 11.9.2: dauuricae (genitive) = dauurica (nominative), or fell under ICZN (Code 1985) Art. 11 (h) (ii)."
  • The significance of italics and nonitalics remains of interest. I summarize Michael Walters' email note discussing this:
    • Pallas is a binomial work. His species no.226 on p.77 of vol.2 is Tetrao Perdix. Under this work he describes two varieties of the species.
      • "Varietatis rupestris dauuricae"
      • "Varietatem albam" (which is presumably an albino).
      It seems clear from this [second instance] that he was intending to describe a colour variety, not a taxon, thus Michael interprets that Peters' conclusion, though not his citation, [regarding "dauurica"] would appear to be correct, (i.e. that the name was not proposed in a nomenclatural sense). According to Ogilvie-Grant (Cat. Birds, 23, p.192) the only subsequent use of Pallas's name prior to Verreaux & Des Murs name barbata (1863) was Schrenck (1859) Reise. Amur-L., i, p.521.
      [END of summary of Michael's email]
    • "Varietatis" would appear to clearly not be a name in a nomenclatural sense, and is in italics.
    • "rupestris" and "albam" (not in italics) also appear not to be names in an nomenclatural sense.
    • "dauuricae" (in italics) is (now) treated as a name in a nomenclatural sense, though possibly not so intended originally.
    • Thus it would appear that nomenclatural intent in this work, as in many others, can not be inferred simply from the order of the words or the italicization.
  • The issue of the number of u's in dauurica appears to have settled upon dauurica as the accepted form, though the basis for the controversy or the resolution is not at the moment known to me.
