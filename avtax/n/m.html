Mathewsia genus group name Nomenclature
  • Antigone Reichenbach 1852 is recently proposed as a genus group name to deal with the paraphyly identified in Grus. (See Krajewski, et al.; Auk 127:440-452 & NACC 2016-C4)
  • However Antigone was used by J.E. Gray (1847) in a list of molluscs. PZS 15:184
  • Iredale (1911) discussed the possible use of Antigone, and proposed Mathewsia with the type Ardea rubicunda Perry 1817. (Iredale's 1911 BBOC article can be seen here: BBOC 27:47)
  • However, in 1914 (Austral.Av.Rec. 2 no.5 p.81) Iredale noted that a Coleopteran genus "Mathewsia" had been erected in 1868, and he proposed a replacement name Mathewsena with the same type. The 1914 Austral.Av.Rec. article can be seen here: AAR1914_81 & AAR1914_82
  • Wolters (1982) lists Mathewsena as a subgenus of Grus.
  • I presume the gender here is feminine.
  • In the NACC proposal to use Antigone I do not find any discussion of the molluscan use of the name, or of Iredale's proposed use of Mathewsena

  • Schlegel (1863) in Mus.Hist.Pays-BasRev.Meth.Crit.Coll. livr.4 no.22 Procellariae p.15,16 listed components of Procellaria nivea including Bonaparte's major, and here includes measurements and specifying descriptive features.
  • I interpret Schlegel as using this name as a subspecies within Procellaria nivea, and being the first to meet the requirements for a valid description. Therefore, I list the authority in parentheses.

Dicaeum melanozanthum Nomenclature
  • This name was originally written by Blyth as "P[achyglossa] melanozantha Hodgson"
  • Finn Salomonsen writes (Peters CL 12:179 f.n.1) 
    1 The spelling melanozantha is unintentional, since everywhere else
in the paper Blyth uses the spelling "-xantha" where this vocable
enters a new species name (such as Rhipidura hypoxantha). Further-
more, Blyth subsequently used the spelling Pachyglossa melanoxantha,
e.g., in 1849, Cat. Birds Mus. Asiat. Soc., p. 339. — F. S.
  • I think Salomonsen would have been more correct if he had said that his opinion was that the spelling was unintentional.
  • In my opinion the initial spelling "melanozantha" probably was intentional, and represented the name as communicated to Blyth by Hodgson (in a letter, in a manuscript, on a specimen label, or possibly on a drawing or painting). In support of this possibility is the fact that Hodgson used a "z" rather than an "x" in a similar naming situation (Coccythraustus melanozanthos Hodgson 1836, now Mycerobas melanozanthos).
  • Salomonsen cites Blyth's spelling of Rhipidura hypoxantha in support of his assertion that the spelling melanozantha was unintentional, and a casual superficial consideration of the matter would motivate one to agree. However the name Rhipidura hypoxantha apparently is a name due to Blyth and Blyth alone (no attribution to Hodgson is given). Thus it seems to me that Blyth is consistent in his habit of latinizing this vocable to "x" rather than "z". This instance does not support Salomosen's point which must address Blyth's treatment of a name proposed or used by Hodgson. Rather, it seems to me that Blyth was fastidious, careful, and intentional in his treatment of this vocable, and there is every reason to presume that he was similarly careful in the rendering "melanozanthum Hodgson".
  • By this reasoning, I believe the spelling should be melanozanthum.

Macgregoria Citation
  • The Genus and the species pulchra are often cited to Ibis(7) III no.10 1897 p.251 pl.VIII.
  • The Ibis material was published in April of 1897, but this species was first described and the genus group name used in Jan. 1897 in a brief note in the BBOC, where it is indicated that DeVis was responsible for the name and the description.
  • The actual wording of the BBOC report is:
    	Mr. Sclater exhibited a specimen of a new Paradise-bird sent to be figured in 'The Ibis' 
	by Mr. De Vis and proposed to be named Macgregoria pulchra. It had large eye-wattles 
	like Paradigalla carunculata, but quite different in shape, and the front was not naked, 
	but covered with erect bristles. This species had been discovered by Sir William Macgregor 
	on Mount Scratchley during his recent expedition across British New Guinea at an 
	altitude of from 10,000 to 12,000 feet.
  • The grammar here is short of ideal, but I think it is reasonable to interpret the first sentence as: "Mr. Sclater exhibited a specimen of a new Paradise-bird sent to be figured in 'The Ibis' by Mr. De Vis and proposed [by him] to be named Macgregoria pulchra.
  • Thanks to Edward Dickinson for bringing this to my attention and for his communication of Richard Schodde's interpretation of the matter (which also supports De Vis as the author, and the BBOC as the citation).

Myospiza humeralis manimbe Nomenclature
  • Often cited to either:
    1. Ammodramus xanthornus Gould 1839 Zool.Voy.Beagle[Darwin] pt11 p.90 pl.30
    2. Ammodramus xanthornus Darwin 1839 Zool.Voy.Beagle[Darwin] pt11 p.90 pl.30
    and of these, the Gould citation appears to be correct, as the publication of Gould's plate evidently preceeded the publication of the text by four months. (ref. Steinheimer, Dickinson, & Walters. 2006. "The zoology of the voyage of the HMS Beagle. Part III. Birds: new avian names, their authorship and their dates" BBOC 126(3):171-193.).
  • However, I am not convinced, as Steinheimer et al. appear to be, that the correct name for this subspecies is Myospiza humeralis xanthornus (Gould). Rather, it appears to me that Myospiza humeralis manimbe (Lichtenstein, 1823) may be correct. Steinheimer et al. state that the "substitute name Ammodramus manimbe (Gray, 1839) is a junior synonym." but this statement has problems of its own.
    1. The reference "(Gray, 1839)" is not clear to me -- are they referring to Gray's contribution to the "Zool.Voy.Beagle" ?? (the only Gray reference they cite is Gray 1840 List of Genera of Birds.
    2. It is not clear to me why this is a "replacement name", but I have not seen Gray's entry.
    3. From various sources including the CBBM 12:691, it appears likely that the combination used by Gray was Ammodramus manimbe and thus the use of parentheses around the date and Gray's name here would be incorrect.
    4. It does not address the more important issue of the status of Fringilla manimbe Lichtenstein, 1823.
    Additionally it must be noted that the card in the Richmond Index for Ammodramus xanthornus Gould has a note saying:
    		Suppressed in favor of A. manimbe.
    though I can so far find no evidence of this suppression.
  • Lichtenstein's 1823 name is cited by Ridgway 1898 as the type for Myospiza, and is usually cited appropriately as a junior synonym of Tanagra humeralis Bosc 1792. This fact alone does not make the name unavailable for a subspecies.
  • The question then becomes if the Lichtenstein name can be applied to the same population as the Gould name, and if so it clearly has priority by 16 years.
  • The Lichtenstein specimen is listed as coming from "Bahia", and it is difficult to know exactly what that meant in 1823. Today, it refer to a State in central to southern Brazil.
  • Chapman 1912 Bull.Am.Mus.Nat.Hist. vol.31 Article XVI p.162 describes specimens of Myospiza manimbe maninbe both from Bahia (the type locality) and much farther south and west from Chapada in the state of Matto Grosso, fairly close to and actually south of north eastern Bolivia, which is the range indicated by H&M 3rd:785 for Myospiza humeralis xanthornus Gould.
  • It appears to me that the systematic problem with this species is great, and resolution of the nomenclatural issues must await progress on the systematic front.
  • The Gould xanthornus name may well be the appropriate one for this population, but only if manimbe Lichtenstein is shown to be a synonym of humeralis Bosc, and not a senior synonym of xanthornis Gould.

  • Arcese, P., M. K. Sogge, A. B. Marr, and M. A. Patten. 2002. Song Sparrow (Melospiza melodia). In The Birds of North America, No. 704 (A. Poole and F. Gill, eds.). The Birds of North America, Inc., Philadelphia, PA. comment:

  • Actually Fringilla cinerea is nowhere mentioned by Oberholser in his 1899 Auk note, where he writes:
    Fringilla guttata Nuttall (Man. Orn., ed. 2, 1840, I, 581), which is now
Melospiza fasciata guttata, is debarred by Fringilla guttata Vieillot
(Nouv. Dict. d'Hist. Nat., 1817, XII, 233), for an Australian Weaver-bird.
As the Rusty Song Sparrow seems to have no other name, it may be
called Melospiza melodia morphna.
  • The place the name Fringilla cinerea Audubon plays in this story appears to be as follows. The type of Melospiza melodia morphna Oberholser is specimen #1860 in the USNM collection. It was collected by John K. Townsend, and received into the collection from Spencer Baird. It was in Baird's private collection and he had in turn acquired it from Edward Harris, who had himself gotten it either from Audubon or Townsend. A different Townsend specimen from his "Columbia river" material (tag # 1942) had also been in Baird's private collection, and Baird had written on the label 
    "Fringilla cineara of Audubon. Type of his description and figure."
  • Friedmann's summary (p.664) is:
      Melospiza melodia morphna Oberholser is a new name for guttata Nuttall,
preoccupied in Fringilla, and is based on the same type specimen(s).

Centropus menbeki Nomenclature
  • The question here is whether the specific epithet should be spelled menbeki or menebiki. There seems to be agreement that the name is spelled menbeki on pl.33 and menebiki in the text (p.600). So it is a question of publishing history and which was published first, the text or the plate.
  • First we look to the Cat.B.Br.Mus. 1891 19:336 where we have (selected)
    1. Centropus menbeki, Less. & Garn. Voy. Coq., Zool. Atlas, pl.33 (1826)
    2. Centropus menebikii, Garn. Voy. Coq., Zool. i. pt.2, p.600 (1828)
    3. Centropus menbikii, Less. Man. Orn. ii. p.122 (1828)
    4. Centropus menebiki, Less. Traité, p.137 (1831)
    As we will see below, the 1826 date for the plate is wrong, and is 1828.
  • Sherborn Index Animalium (p.4000) lists:
    • menbiki, Centropus, R.P. Lesson, Man. Orn. II. June 1828 122.
      • We will see that this Man.Orn. entry is also antedated (March 1828) by the plate, but in fact antedates the text name (Nov. 1829) which is proposed as having priority by the Voisin work (v.i.).
  • The Richmond Index lists:
    • Centropus menbeki Prosper Garnot. 1828 Voyage autour du monde, La Coquille; Zoologie (livr.6) Mch. 1828, pl.33.
      Text, tome I , pt. II, livr. 13, Nov. 1829, 600 where C. Menebiki.
  • Peters Checklist 1940 4:67 where the name is listed as menbeki citing Voy. Coquille Atlas, 1828, pl.33; Zool., 1, livr. 13, 1829, p.6001 and the footnote reads:
    	1 The specific name is spelled menebiki in the text, and menebikii
on the table of plates accompanying the Atlas.
  • Then we have Voisin C & Voisin J-F. 1999. "Liste des types d'oiseaux des collections du Muséum national d'Histoire naturelle de Paris. 6. Coucous (Cuculidae)." Zoosystema 21(2):389 where the entry reads:
    Centropus Menebiki Garnot in Lesson & 
Garnot, 1828.
	Garnot in Lesson & Garnot, Voyage autour du
	Monde exécuté par ordre du Roi sur la Corvette de
	Sa Maj. La Coquille pendant années 1822,
	1823, 1824 et 1825. Zoologie 1: 600 (1828), 
	Atlas, pl.33 (1838).
	Centropus menebiki menebiki Garnot, 1828.
	(=Centropus menbeki menbeki auct.)
    Quite substantial differences here from other authors.

Many studies and much careful work has been done and published on this publication; the easiest authoritative source to consult is Zimmer 1926 p.186-188 which benefits from prior work by Sherborn and Mathews and includes those results. Zimmer makes it clear that the records of the "Bibliographie de la France" as well as data from Férussac's "Bull. de Sciences Naturelles" lead to the understanding that the Voisin's interpretation of dates, and thus which name has priority, can not be regarded as correct. We know from Mathews discovery in Férussac's "Bull. de Sciences Naturelles" that the date of publication for plate 33 (in livraison 6) was 22 March 1828 (a Saturday). Livraison 13 (containing pp.561-600 and the text name) was published Nov. 21, 1829 (also a Saturday), almost a year and a half after the plate. This is known from the records of the "Bibliographie de la France".
  • Voisin & Voisin refer to Sherborn & Woodward 1906. Ann.Mag.Nat.Hist.(7) 17:335-336, but not to the additional later work by Mathews. 1913. AustralAvianRecord 2(3,4):49-54, nor to Zimmer.
  • The plate name Centropus menbeki has priority over the text name C. Menebiki.
  • My interpretation of Zimmer indicates that the authority for the name (on the plate) is to Lesson & Garnot, and not to Garnot in Lesson & Garnot.

    • This name is attributed to Boie in 1844 Isis 37 col.188, note. and it appears that this tradition of reference was established by Saunders in Cat.B.Br.Mus. 25:145 and followed by Mathews.
    • In a letter from CW Richmond to Witmer Stone (1915.02.05) some interesting questions are raised.
      1. Richmond indicates that Boie describes a Sterna minutus from the collection of Prince Essling (and from some British Museums) "which is a species of Anous or Micronous."
      2. Richmond also asserts that the Essling specimen is allegedly from California and thus may not be Australian.
      3. Richmond indicates that Boie lists his species of Anous with the abbreviation St. and "dittos" all the species which suggests to Richmond that Boie intended to call his new species Sterna minutus (a name which is, of course, preoccupied by Sterna minuta Linnaeus 1766 = S. albifrons Pallas 1764). -- but see below regarding St..
      4. Richmond regards the Essling specimen as the type (as it is the only "definite one" that Boie mentions).
      5. If the Essling specimen is from California it is possibly not related to the form that Matthews has in mind.
      6. If the specimen does relate to one of the forms Matthews has in mind, the name may be preoccupied, and Richmond suggests in such a case Anous leucocapillus Gould may be the appropriate available name.
    • The Richmond Index, in fact, lists a card for Anous minutus Boie (!), but it does not appear to be in Richmond's hand, and cites the type locality as determined by Mathews.

      minuta  Sternula (L.), F. Boie, Isis (Oken), 1822, 563. -- Sterna, 1766.
      Suggesting that Boie may also possibly have been referring to his previous use of Sternula minuta.
    • The Richmond Index has a card for the genus group name Sternula F. Boie Isis (Oken) Bd. X Heft V, col.563 1822, and gives the type as "minuta".
    • So all this is somewhat confusing, but it appears to me (without having seen any of the primary sources) [but see below] that Boie in 1844 was listing the species of Anous and included there names that he had previously held to be in "St." -- presumably his Sternula. Thus it may well be appropriate to list the authority without parentheses if the type specimen is in fact not from California, as the Essling specimen would appear to be.
    • If the type specimen for this name is not from the Australasian region the entire taxon would appear to be problematic.
    • It appears to me that further investigation of this matter is appropriate.

    Centrocercus minimus Nomenclature
    • I previously followed the AOU. 42nd supplement to the AOU Checklist Auk 2000. 117(3):850. for the citation, which was:
      • Centrocercus minimus Bradbury & Vehrencamp 1998 PrincipalsAnimalCommunication cover p.iv,770
    • I now follow the AOU. 43rd supplement to the AOU Checklist Auk 2002. 119(3):899.
    • A complex and unfortunate situation exists here. I understand this name, though unpublished, was used informally for some time. The Bradbury and Vehrencamp publication of the name was not intended as the species description, but nonetheless was interpreted by Banks, and others who have reviewed the data and the applicable rules, as satisfying the ICZN rules then in effect, and was thus, whether advertent or not, a valid species description.
    • Shortly after that publication, the new 4th ICZN rules came into effect and the description would NOT then have satisfied the criteria.
    • Those who worked on recognition of the taxon published their work in the last 2000 issue of The Wilson Bulletin, where they described it as as "new species", and did NOT cite the Bradbury and Vehrencamp work, OR the AOU checklist update's use of the name as a Bradbury and Vehrencamp taxon.
    • I understand that there was some consideration to petition the ICZN to suppress the Bradbury and Vehrencamp work (which had unquestioned priority) and thus have their work (being the next available) as the citation.
    • The action of the AOU Checklist Committee may make this moot. The Checklist Committee held that the name was used by Vehrencamp and Bradbury as "a means of temporary reference and not for formal taxonomic use as a scientific name in zoological nomenclature," and thus was excluded under ICZN 1985 Art.1(b)(6) and ICZN 1999 Art. 1.3.5.
    • This would appear to be a lesson and a motivator to remember the importance of publishing and establishing valid names so they can be used by the scientific community in a uniform and consistent manner.
    • An additional point:
      • I can not understand Young et al.'s motivation for NOT mentioning either Bradbury & Vehrencamp, OR the initial findings of the AOU Checklist Committee in their WilsonBull. article of description. I can understand their disappointment, and concern over the problem, but by not mentioning the issue in their WilsonBull. paper they ensure that the nomenclatural issue will be more difficult to understand when people in the future look back on this name. In addition, in my view, it produces the possibility of an interpretation of petulance, rather than a desire to resolve and clarify a nomenclatural issue.

[Taxonomy of Great Horned Owls (Strigidae: Bubo spp.) in southern South 
America.]

According to Amadon & Bull (1988) there is only one Bubo species in the whole
American continent from Alaska to Tierra del Fuego: Bubo virginianus. Traylor
[Auk 75: 143-148 (1958)] studied variation and races of the Great Horned Owl in 
South America and came to the conclusion that the form east of the Andes must be 
Bubo virginianus nacurutu, whereas the birds of Patagonia, Chile, and of the
Andes from Tierra del Fuego to Peru should be called B. v. magellanicus. Indeed,
nacurutu and magellanicus are rather different: they have very obvious
differences in bill and foot dimensions, and in the barring of the underparts. 
Fjeldsa & Krabbe (1990) used the name magellanicus for the smaller taxon from
Patagonia and the southern Andes.
It would be a surprise that a subspecies occuring in sub-arctic Patagonia and 
the High Andes is smaller than a [sister] subspecies living in warmer climatic
conditions, and indeed, compared to other subsp. of virginianus, magellanicus is
a dwarf form. Besides these morphological differences, the vocalizations of both 
Bubo forms are quite distinct. The Great Horned Owl is called 'nacurutú' in
Argentinia, but 'tucúquere' in Chile, apparently an onomatopetic transcription
of the calls of these birds.

Morphologic and bioacoustic comparison of the forms magellanicus and nacurutu.
B. magellanicus is small, has a relatively small bill (culmen under 43 mm) and
comparatively small feet. Below, this taxon [is] barred very narrowly light and dark.
There are light morphs showing much white, but there are also rather dark morphs 
with a brownish main color.
The wings are, according to Traylor, in males from Chile and Argentinia on 
average 329,0 mm (318-339), in females 342,9 mm (330-358). Birds from the Andes 
of Bolivia and Peru are somewhat larger (wings 351-380 mm); the bill is on 
average 38,0 mm in males and 39,3 mm in females. Birds in the Stuttgart 
collection agree with these measurements.
If one compares living birds side by side, so the different culmen and talon 
measurements are very obvious [ther are photos of pairs of both forms in the 
paper which show these differences very impressive!]. Further on, both taxa have 
different 'faces' and feather ears (longer in nacurutu).
Still more impressive are the differences in songs of both taxa. Both sexes of 
magellanicus sing in duet, the male uttering a double-call "wu-bh" with a
quiet tremolo on "o": "wu-bh-worrrr". 'Tucquere' is a good circumscription of 
this vocalization. The female sings similarily, but the tremolo is a little 
longer.
The nacurutu subsp. of virginianus is a little larger than magellanicus, with
large feet and a stronger bill. Barring is not as narrow and the facial 
expression is more "wild", caused by the longer feather ears and the strong bill.
Wing length in 15 males is 341,4 mm on average (330-354), in 21 females 363,1 mm 
(240-376); the strong bill is about 45,6 mm in 14 males and 48,8 mm in 20 females.
Pairs vocalize in duets also. The song of males is like "wu-bubú buh buh" in
females like "wu-bububú" followed by a drawn-out "buh". The first part of the
females song is fairly good circumscribed with 'nja-curutú'.

Molecular biological results
Heidrich and Wink conducted a sequence analysis of the mt cytochrome b genes in 
their laboratory. Material was used from blood and feathers and by PCR they 
amplified 960 base pairs. Nucleotide sequences differed in 15 positions (1,6%), 
according to about 800 000 years when using the 2% rule. Both taxa are 
apparently sibling species, comparable to the differentiation of Spotted and 
Lesser Spotted Eagles (Aquila clanga, A. pomarina) or the Imperial and Spanish
Imperial Eagles (A. heliaca, A. adalberti).

Discussion
As vocalizations are likely the most important mechanisms of isolation in owls, 
it seems highly probable that B. magellanicus and B. virginianus are good
species. Genetic distances not only confirm this assumption, but also confirm 
the hypothesis that vocalizations in owls are of special importance for owl 
taxonomy.
The form nacurutu apparently represents a subspecies of Bubo virginianus,
distributed in North and most of South America. It occurs in southern South 
America east of the Andes, where it has its southern limits in the Province of 
Buenos Aires.
Bubo magellanicus is relatively common in Tierra del fuego and Patagonia, where
it lives in mountainous habitats. There it occurs in loosely wooded areas of 
southern beech as well as in mountains. It is distributed northward on the
Pacific slope (Chile) as well as in the central part (high-andine) of the Andes 
of Peru, agreeing with Traylor's findings of magellanicus. In the eastern slopes
of the Andes an overlapping zone of both species may be found, where B.
magellanicus apparently prefers rocky areas.

Comments on taxonomy
New World Bubo are an interesting group with a complicated taxonomy, determined 
by the following factors:
        

      • a. with some exceptions, there is relatively few material of these birds
available. In zoos and in private collections of living birds the number of 
individuals and subspecies is limited.
        • 

      • b. polymorphy, the existence of two or more color morphs, shows parallel
variation among subspecies; this means that there are very similarely colored
birds in different distrubutional areas. The scale light-intermediate-dark birds 
is often shifted from one subspecies to the other. The light morph of one subsp. 
can be similar to the intermediate or dark morphs of another subsp.
        • 

      • c. a number of well described subspecies, of relatively small populations, is
surrounded often by a number of variants occupying a larger area.
        • 

      • d. there are broad zones of intergradation with intermediate populations between
races.

Comments on distribution, size and coloration
The species is distributed from the west coast of Alaska, over subarctic boreal 
areas of Canada, parts of the tundras of Canada and Labrador, to Middle and 
South America and Tierra del Fuego. It is lacking on the Queen Charlotte Is., 
the West Indies, and larger areas of the South American lowland rain forests.
Some insular areas of occurence within these areas are little known. There are 
some obvious clines in size and coloration in agreement with Bergmann's and 
Gloger's rules. 

Color morphs and intermediate populations
As mentioned above, each subspec. has its typical morph, but there are one or 
more other morphs detectable. Often these morphs agree well with morphs of other 
subsp., but not always in size and proportions. There are two color plates 
showing the different morphs and subspecies, and two maps depicting the 
distribution of the subsp.
Checking of the collecting localities and breeding places revealed that several 
described subsp. are only morphs of other subsp. So is lagophonus only a synonym
of saturatus (the latter is given as restricted to a small coastal area in
western North America, but it occurs also in the interior of Brit. Columbia). 
Weick found large areas of secondary contact between the forms wapacuthu,
heterocnemis, occidentalis and virginianus, where intermediate populations are
found. Smaller areas of introgrssion are known between virginianus and
pallescens, pallescens and pacificus, and pacificus and saturatus.

      Porphyrio is masculine. The word martinicensis is definitely adjectival (masculine and feminine; neuter: martinicense).
