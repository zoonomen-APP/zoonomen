    Gallirallus striatus obscurior Nomenclature
    • As Colin Jones points out (in litt. 2010.04.17) the original spelling of the epithet is obscuriora. So questions arise concerning the proper rendering of the name when the taxon is held in the masculine Gallirallus.
    • Normand David notes (in litt. 2010.04.19) that the case is a bit of a puzzle. He indicates that the form obscurior would be correct as the masculine or the feminine adjectival, but the question is not currently resolved as to whether a change to obscurior is in fact correct.

    2008.03.29 (RMR)
    Oriolus Systematics
    • I found the H&M 3rd treatment of Oriolus problematic. In some instances (e.g. the treatment of Sphecotheres viridis, hypoleucus, vielloti, flaviventris) the nature of agreement with and departure from the Peters Checklist treatment is explained and referenced. In other instances (e.g. recognition of Oriolus melanotis, and lack of recognition of Oriolus viridifuscus) changes are not mentioned, discussed or referenced. In these cases it is not clear if the change results from nomenclature or whether the changed listing represents a systematic decision. I believe that H&M 3rd does not pretend to be, and can not be regarded as a systematic work.
    • Edward Dickinson (in litt. 2008.01.26) indicates that the treatment of Oriolidae follows the presentation in Dickinson et al. SNAB 42 & 43, though this is not mentioned in H&M 3rd.

2006.07.20


    

Cyanomitra obscura Citation

      

    • Cited by Peters Checklist 12:245 to 1843 and this is followed universally.
      • 

    • Sheets-Pyenson, Susan. 1981. "War and Peace in Natural History Publishing: The Naturalist's
Library, 1833-1843." ISIS 72:50-72. addresses this work in detail. From her study of
Jardine's correspondence she determines that this work was published in Dec. 1842.
      • 

    • I am unaware of any reason not to believe her interpretation, which appears to have been
universally ignored by the ornithological community.
      • 

    • Iredale also published on this work (Iredale. 1951. "'The Naturalist's Library: an Essay in Bibliography.'
Australian Zoologist 11:322-332.) and Edward Dickinson kindly provided me a copy to study. It is clear that
uncertainty remains as to whether this volume was published in Dec. 1842, or in 1843. Possible explanations can be
provided for either case. The appearance (but not the certainty) is that Sheets-Pyenson may have dated this based on
information in the Jardine correspondence (which was material unknown to Iredale), and Sheets-Pyenson was fully aware of
and relied partly Iredale's work when she published her paper. For the present time I am employing the dating she
published on p.72 of her work.

Ortalis Nomenclature


      


    • A confusing situation exists regarding the relationship between 
Ortalis and Ortalida.
      • 


    • It appears that both names can be attributed to Merrem in 1786, though
Ortalida is also attributed to Cuvier 1817 (=1816) and J.J. Fleming 1822.
      • 


    • The cards in the Richmond Index, include entries for both Ortalida and Ortalis
attributed to Merrem. There is a note stating "properly used in the nominative form
of Ortalis. Additionally one card for Ortalis Merrem, has a note
"(not in Germ. Ed.!)".
      • 


    • Thus it appears that Ortalis and Ortalida can be regarded as different grammatical
forms of the same word, and further that some confusion may result from one form (Ortalis) 
not being present in some editions (while present in others).
      • 

    • Ortalis mccalli Baird, and Ortalis cinereiceps Gray, were both described
in Ortalida, which for nomenclatural purposes, I regard as equivalent to Ortalis.

    • Originally spelled "Ocrophus" by Linnaeus, and this spelling is followed by 
Peters Checklist 2:267 (1934).

    • It appears there was concern about the spelling for some time, as for example Mathews in 1913

    • However, ochropus has been officially recognised and ocrophus officially rejected by ICZN under 
their Direction 17 of 14 July 1955 (erroneously listed by Sibley & Monroe, 1990:237 as 1952 [sic]).  
      • 

    • One notes, with some amusement that the now accepted spelling (ochropus) is that which Gmelin employed in 1789, but Linnaeus still gets awarded the authority.
