Chlorostilbon Type species
  • There is a lack of consensus in the literature regarding the typification of this Genus.
  • The AOU CL 6th 1983 and 7th 1998 give "Type, by monotypy, Chlorostilbon prasinus Gould (not of authors) = Trochilus pucherani Bourcier = Ornismya aureo-ventris d'Orbigny and Lafresnaye."
  • Then in 2017 we have "The generic classification of the Trochilini (Aves: Trochilidae):Reconciling taxonomy with phylogeny" 2017. Stiles,FG; Remsen,JV; & McGuire, JA. Zootaxa 4353:401-424, where they list (Appendix 1. p.418) the type as "Ornismya prasinus (sic) Lesson, 1830, Hist.Nat.Ois.Mouch., 188 (=Trochilus mellisuga Linnaeus, 1758, Syst. Nat.,ed. 10, 121

  • Before proceeding to the disagreement, I address one minor detail.
    • Lesson's taxon is Ornismya prasina, not Ornismya prasinus This can be seen here: Hist.Nat.Ois-M. p.188. This fact (commonly confused, and indeed confused by Gould) has no effect on the subsequent considerations.

  • In addressing the matter of the type, the Stiles group appears (to me) to be following the arguments of John Todd Zimmer. They cite two papers by Zimmer in the Am.Mus.Novitates, "Studies of Peruvian Birds, nos.58 & 59." In the first of these Am.Mus.Novitates 1474 (not 1473, as they list it), Zimmer has a long argument in which he states that Lesson's bird, can in fact be identified as the bird that Linnaeus called Trochilus mellisugus.
    • He states (p.5) "I have no hesitation in placing Ornismya prasina of Lesson as a synonym of Trochilus mellisugus of Linnaeus..."
    • But in my view, assigning the specimen that Lesson called Ornismya prasina to Trochilus mellisugus Linnaeus, based on the Linnaeus and Lesson descriptions is important only if Gould erected his genus based on that specimen.
    • In fact, Gould apparently based his genus on some specimen, that some collector called Ornismya prasina, apparently a commonly used name with unknown relation to Lesson's concept.

  • Thus the point, apparently missed by Zimmer (!) and the Stiles group, is that the type of Chlorostilbon Gould 1853, while intended to be Ornismya prasina Lesson, may or may not have been that species, but rather Gould simply applied it to a bird "generally known by that term among collectors..." not a bird necessarily so named by Lesson. In such a situation the phrase "not of authors", is appropriate.

  • Other presentations of the type for Chlorostilbon.
    • Cat.BirdsBr.Mus. 1892 16:44 C. pucherani
    • Cat.BirdsAm. Cory & Hellmayr 2.1:202 Ornismya prasina Lesson
    • BirdsMiddleN.AM. Ridgway 5:547 Ornismya prasina Lesson
    • Peters CL Birds of the World Peters,JL 1945 5:36 Chlorostilbon prasina Gould, not of authors = Trochilus pucherani Bourcier.
  • In Gould's Monograph of the Trochilidae, he discusses this bird (p.cx), indicating it was "impossible to determine to what bird Lesson had given the name prasinus (sic)"
    He (Gould) applied it to a bird "generally known by that term among collectors..." "From Dr. Cabanis we learn that it has been named Trochilus nitidissimus by Lichtenstein in the Museum of Berlin, and Trochilus lamprus, Natt. in the Museum of Munich ; but had either of these names been published to the world before Dr. Cabanis included it in his 'Museum Heineanum' under the name of Chlorostilbon nitidissimus ? If not, and prasinus be rejected, that term must certainly give place to M. Bourcier's previously published one of Pucherani, which I find, from the type specimen now before me, was given to a young male of this species. Refer to my account of this species, and of C. Atala."

  • So from Gould's perspective Pucherani was conditionally the type, if two conditions are met:
    • 1. prasin[a,us] Lesson not being identifiable (Gould says it was not identifiable to him)., and
    • 2. Trochilus nitidissimus having not been previously published for the bird.
  • Does it now turn on were met vs. are met, and does it make Gould's inability to identify whether or not the bird was Lesson's Ornismya prasina unimportant?

Curruca Nomenclature
  • This previously much used genus group name has been resurrected for use in dealing with the deep and multiple splits in the Sylviidae.
  • To date (2020.08.17) I have not found any published discussion of the nomenclature involved, and at least I find a number of questions.
  • First, the name has been used, it seems in a generic sense, before its use by Bechstein 1802, which is proposed (without comment) as the authority for the name.
  • Some uses, e.g. by Koelreuter in 1767, appear to have been held to be non-binomial. I know of no comments on whether Moehring's 1758 use is binomial. Naumann in 1797 I vol.1 Heft.5 pl.33 fig.68 may have used it generically, but again I find no comment on this.
  • Additionally questions arise regarding the type. Generally this would (I expect) be assumed to be Sylvia curruca Linnaeus 1758, but in 1830, Jardine and Selby provide an illustration of Sylvia atricapilla Latham saying it is "typical of the genus". Is this typification by subsequent designation? Probably not, but did anyone ask, or look into it?"
  • If all this has been investigated I am possibly just kicking up dust because I don't find the relevant literature.
  • Sherborn's Index Animalium has 85 taxa employing the name Carruca before 1850. A name that has been used in lots of ways by many different people and in many different contexts. Lots of opportunity for trouble it seems to me.
  • No doubt the authors resurrecting this old, old name have done due diligence and investigated all the possible problems that may lurk in the past, and they have spared us from reading of their work.

    The description of the species figured on the plates was afterwards undertaken by Audoin (sic),

de la Syrie" in Part 4 of Vol. I of the Hist. Nat. of the Description de l'Égypte."

Dicrurus bracteatus carbonarius Nomenclature
  • Those of us who struggle with the twin challenges of getting nomenclature correct and editing nomenclatural texts should take heart from the fact that the most renowned published authors were challenged as well. Volume 15 of the Peters Checklist has many instructive examples of editorial lapses and nomenclaturial inconsistencies. Familiarity with the fact that error and inconsistency in this volume should be expected suggests that the most appropriate response to these infelicities is silence, never forgetting the difficulty of the task and the great advances provided by these authors.
  • The character of the errors prompts one to speculate on how the work flow, and editing responsibilities were actually carried out.
  • One example that catches my attention, from a nomenclatural point of view relates to the treatments of Dicrurus hottentotus carbonarius and Dicrurus hottentotus densus. In the Peters Checklist these taxa are found on pp.149,150, and both names are from Bonapartes Consp.Gen.Av. 1. A slightly slippery nomeclatureal point is at play here (I ignore the question as to whether the work should be dated to 1850 or 1851). For both names in Bonaparte's work, the orthography is the directly comparable. This is a scan of the section in question:

    As you can see they are printed as 

    Edolius carbonarius
Edolius densus
    Both of these names actually occur in a section (that starts on p.351) headed Dicrourus and Bonaparte (we interpret) holds all these taxa to be in Dicrourus (the name is simply a variant of Dicrurus).

    I find no evidence of this pipit name.
  • Sherborn Index Animalium 1924 p.1006 has an entry for

    which appears to be junior to the Blyth publication, in a different publication, and with a different spelling!!.
  • Peters Checklist 3:246 errs in the belief that the bird was named for a Miss Calthorp. In this, he cites, and is following the error in Sharpe's Hand-List 1900 2:32
  • Dickinson et al. write (H&M 3rd:[828]):
    The lapsus by Blyth was emended in or before Salvadori (1891); this taxon was named for a Miss
Calthrop and not as Peters (1937) wrote a Miss Calthorp. Use of this accurate emendation from 1891 
to 1937, has been succeeded by over 50 years of use of calthorpae. Sibley & Monroe (1990)
resurrected calthropae, but we judge that calthorpae must benefit from the rules on 
prevailing usage.
  • The definition of "prevailing usage" from the glossary of the ICZN 1999 is as follows:
    usage, prevailing, n.
    Of a name: that usage of the name which is adopted by at least a substantial majority of
    the most recent authors concerned with the relevant taxon, irrespective of how long ago
    their work was published.
  • So, in my view, a demonstration of "prevailing usage" must publish the results of a study showing what universe of data was examined, why that universe of data is sufficient and appropriate to answer the question of frequency of use by different authors, and show that the data demonstrate a substantial majority (i.e. number) of authors used a particular rendering of a name. The argument must include logic in support of the fact that the "majority" is a substantial majority, and not just a simple majority.
    The discussion by Dickinson et al. does not indicte the range of publications and authors consisdered, it does not demonstrate (or even mention) a majority, let alone a "substantial majority" of authors, nor does it indicate criteria for deciding which authors were "concerned with the taxon" (whatever that means). The argument in support of "prevailing usage" appears to be based upon the recent duration of use, while the definition requires establishing a frequency of use (substantial majority of authors concerned with the taxon). It may well be that the proposed prevailing usage can be demonstrated, but in my view, it must be published and the component criteria must be presented with supporting logic and arguments. Until such a time I interpret it to be incompatible with the Code 1999 to use calthorpae.

    15. Palæornis calthropæ [main heading]
	Palæornis calthrapæ Blyth 1849
		[with 9 additional entries from 6 authors following]

  • Conventionally cited to 1887 (e.g. Peters Checklist 9:97 (Mayr & Greenway 1960) ; H&M 3rd:532; Sclater 1930 Syst.Av.Aethiop. II:583).

  • The publishing history of the J.Orn. is vastly rich in evidence of delays and irregularities, and some of the evidences can be regarded as tantamount to proofs, but most, so far, are not.

Chrysothlypis chrysomelaena spelling:

Usually given as chrysomelas. AOU 7th ed. CL uses chrysomelaena,
referencing Deignan, 1961. In that work, Type Specimens of Birds In the
United States National Museum p.588, Deignan makes this argument. 'Since
Hellmayr (loc. cit.) has listed this form as Chrysothlypis chrysomelas
chrysomelas, it follows that he must have considered the generic name
Chrysothlypis masculine. All other names ending with -thlypis, however have
been consistently treated, by himself and others, as feminine (cf.
Geothlypis, Chamaethlypis, and Euthlypis), and Chrysothlypis must therefore
also be feminine.'

[N David continues]
Names ending in -thlypis are not feminine because they are combined with
feminine adjectival name; they are feminine because they end in a
transliterated Greek feminine word [ICZN 1960 30 (a) (ii)]. The rule still
prevails in ICZN (1999, Art. 30.1.2). This means that an original name such
as "Chrysothlypis albus" would need a mandatory correction (= C. alba). In
this case, an author's belief is ignored by the Code.

[Deignan's note continues]
'If this be granted, we must use a latinized form of the feminine ending of
the Greek adjective meaning "black." A comparable case is Turdus protomelas
Cabanis, 1867, which, placed by Seebohm in the genus Merula became Merula
protomelaena (see Catalogue of the birds in the British Museum 5: 265,
1881).'

[N. David's final comment]
In 1960, the rule governing gender agreement of species-group names was not
explicit; in fact, it held in two lines: "an adjective must agree in gender
with the generic name with which it is at any time combined". The 1985 and
1999 Codes, however, are very explicit: an original species-group that is
or ends in a word that is not Latin or latinized must be treated as
indeclinable. Thus, all original names ending in -melas [from melas (black)
in Greek) are invariable, even if melas is an adjective. The rationale
behind this rule is that Latin is the language of scientific nomenclature;
species-group names that are or end in foreign words (neither Latin nor
latinized) cannot have their ending changed as Latin or latinized adjectives."

    Cisticola gender Nomenclature
  • Many specific names were formed, or used with the understandable expectation that the gender of Cisticola was feminine.
  • The ICZN rules are clear: ( ICZN, 1999 p.34)
  • "30.1.1 a genus-group name that is or ends in a Latin word takes the gender given for that word in standard Latin dictionaries; if it is a compound word fromed from two or more components, the gender is given by the final component (in the case of a noun, the gender of that noun; in the case of any other component, sucha as a Latin suffix, the gender appropriate to that component);"
  • In this case the component in question is -cola.
  • The Oxford Latin Dictionary (Glare, ed.) 1982 p.349 gives:
    "-cola ~ae, m. suff. From COLO + A; denotes one who inhabits, tills, or worships."
  • Hence Cisticola is a male gender genus-group name; nana becomes nanus, incana to incanus &c.
